All about problem-solving

by Tiff Wang


I have reached week 4 of my coding journey, and along the way, I have gained valuable insights into problem-solving. I would like to share some of my experiences in problem-solving with my non-tech friend.

When there was a time I was blocked on a simple problem.

The most recent simple problem I encountered was when I attempted to create a function to capitalise each word in a sentence. I quickly wrote down the steps and felt confident in my logic. However, when I tested my code, it failed. I reviewed the code I had written but couldn't identify any issues. I felt stuck and decided to apply the rubber ducky method by speaking the requirement and my solution steps out loud, even though I used my cat instead of a rubber duck!

During the process of explaining the problem step by step, I suddenly realised that I had applied my function to the wrong target. I had assumed that I was given a box of words (we call it an array of words) and needed to capitalize each one. However, what I actually received was a complete sentence (we call it a string). Therefore, I needed to first separate the words and place each word in a box before capitalising them individually, and then assemble all the capitalised words back into a sentence.

Once I added the missing step, the test passed successfully. I found the rubber ducky method, or rather my "cutie cat" method, to be incredibly useful. Sometimes, even when a problem appears simple, it's easy for us to overlook essential details. The act of explaining the problem step by step, whether to a rubber duck or a cat, helps us identify and address those missing pieces.

When there was a time I elegantly solved a problem.

I would like to share another experience where I elegantly solved a problem. I was working on building a cafe order page and adding some new features from the challenge exercises provided in the course. There were a total of 8 questions, and when I reached the 4th question, I stuck. I discovered conflicts between the features, where individual functions designed for specific features would work independently but fail when multiple features were combined.

For instance, I had implemented a feature to display ingredients in red text when they were running out of stock. However, as I added more features, sometimes the stock text would turn red even though there were still 2 items left in stock. To identify the root cause of the problem, I utilised a tool called “console.log” to display the output of my code line-by-line, helping me to identify the exact part that was causing the issue.

Through this exploration, I found that the logic I had used for the first 3 questions was not ideal when more features will come into play. Therefore, Instead of addressing the questions one by one, I tried to read through all 8 questions to gain a comprehensive understanding of the required features and their relationships. Then, I wrote down the steps to create a rough structure of my code and rewrite the code accordingly. I discovered that the coding process became much smoother when I had a clear picture in my mind of the entire project.

This experience taught me the importance of having a holistic view and a draft plan of how the different functions and features will work together when dealing with multiple questions related to the same project.

Reflect on how confident I feel using each of the following problem-solving techniques/processes:

  • Pseudocode
  • Trying something
  • Rubber ducky method
  • Console.logging
  • Asking your peers for help
  • Asking coaches for help
  • Improving your process with reflection

The problem-solving methods I use vary depending on the cases. Based on my coding experience, I found that console.logging, reading error messages, trying something, and googling are effective for most of the problems that I encountered. I have also found pseudocode to be a valuable tool that I recently learned. It allows me to plan ahead and outline the logical steps necessary to solve a problem before diving into the actual coding process. I often use the rubber duck method (my cat) to catch any missed details. If the problem persists, I seek help from peers or coaches. After solving a problem, I usually like to reflect and note down what I’ve learned in a text file for future reference.

Reflect on a time I was reluctant to ask for help.

I have been able to solve the majority of problems through troubleshooting. This means when I am stuck on something, the problem is complex. However, when I encounter those complex issues that are difficult to explain concisely within just short descriptions and screenshots, I feel reluctant to ask for help due to the complexity of describing the problem. But think about the reality that such situation would happen a lot when I work in a company with complex projects. Therefore, when similar complex challenges occur again in the future, I would like to try booking a video call meeting with a coach, allowing us to discuss the problem more effectively by sharing screens.
